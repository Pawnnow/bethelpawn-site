Know Before You Come In

What Affects Your Instrument's Offer

  • 🏷️

    Brand Is Everything

    A Gibson Les Paul and a no-name Les Paul copy look similar but are worlds apart in value. Name brand instruments from Gibson, Fender, Taylor, Martin, and PRS hold resale value. Off-brand instruments are very hard to resell.

  • 🎸

    Playability & Condition

    The instrument must be in playable condition — we'll check tuning stability, fret wear, neck condition, electronics, and action. Instruments that play well get better offers. Cosmetic wear is expected and acceptable.

  • 📦

    Original Case & Accessories

    The original hardshell case adds meaningful value — especially for guitars. Extra pickups, tremolo arms, straps, and original paperwork or hang tags all help.

  • 🔧

    Modifications

    Stock, unmodified instruments typically get better offers than heavily modified ones. Pickup swaps, refrets, and non-original hardware can actually reduce value even if the work was quality.

  • 📊

    Current Resale Market

    Instrument values fluctuate with trends, popularity, and new model releases. We check current market prices — what your guitar is actually selling for today — before making an offer.

💡 Tips to Get the Best Offer on Your Instrument

Change the strings before you come in. Fresh strings on a guitar make a dramatic difference in playability and first impression. A $10 set of strings could meaningfully improve your offer.

Clean it up. Wipe down the body, fretboard, and hardware. A clean instrument shows care and signals it was maintained. This takes 10 minutes and is worth it.

Bring the case. The original hardshell case, if you have it, adds to the offer. A gig bag is less impactful but still worth bringing.

Know the model and year. "1998 Fender American Standard Strat in Olympic White" gets a better response than "a white Fender guitar." Check the headstock decal and the neck plate for serial number — the serial tells us the year.
