Selling Gold

What Pawn Shops Pay for Gold & Jewelry

🏪 Bethel Pawn 📍 Bethel, Ohio ⏱ 7 min read

Gold prices at pawn shops aren't a mystery — they're math. This guide explains exactly how your gold offer is calculated, what factors affect the number, and what you can do to maximize what you get when you sell gold or jewelry near Cincinnati.

The Gold Pricing Formula

Every legitimate pawn shop that buys gold uses a variation of the same formula. There is no magic or mystery to it — it's three variables multiplied together:

Weight (grams) × Purity (karat %) × Spot Price ($/gram)
= Your Gold's Raw Value

The offer you receive will be a percentage of this raw value — typically 70–85% at reputable shops — which covers the dealer's operating costs and margin.

Let's walk through each component.

Weight

Gold is weighed in grams (or sometimes troy ounces — 1 troy oz = 31.1 grams). A legitimate pawn shop uses a precision scale and weighs your item in front of you. If stones or non-gold components are present, those should be weighed separately and excluded from the gold calculation.

Purity (Karat)

Not all gold is the same purity. The karat marking tells you what percentage of the piece is actually gold:

A pawn shop tests purity using an acid test or electronic tester. This takes about 60 seconds and is done in front of you. If a shop doesn't test your gold, that's a red flag.

Spot Price

The "spot price" of gold is the live market price — what gold is trading for right now on commodity exchanges. It fluctuates daily, sometimes significantly. A reputable shop pulls the current spot price before making an offer. If a shop uses a fixed price list that doesn't change with the market, their offers may not reflect actual gold value.

You can look up the current gold spot price yourself at any time — it's publicly available. When you come to Bethel Pawn, we pull the live price and show you the calculation.

A Real Example

Say you have a 14K gold chain weighing 10 grams. At a spot price of $90/gram for pure gold: 10g × 0.583 (14K purity) × $90/gram = $524.70 raw gold value. A fair offer would be roughly $370–$445 (70–85% of raw value), depending on the shop's operating costs and current buying volume.

What About Diamonds & Gemstones?

When your jewelry contains diamonds or other gemstones, the pricing gets more nuanced. The metal value is calculated as above. The stones are assessed separately based on the "4 Cs":

  • Cut — How well the diamond is shaped and faceted. Better cut = more brilliance = higher value.
  • Clarity — The presence or absence of inclusions (internal flaws). Fewer inclusions = higher value.
  • Color — Colorless diamonds are most valuable. Scale runs D (colorless) to Z (noticeably yellow).
  • Carat — The weight of the stone. Larger stones are exponentially more valuable per carat.

Having a GIA (Gemological Institute of America) certificate for your diamond can meaningfully support a higher offer — it gives the buyer verified specs rather than estimates. If you have original paperwork or appraisals, bring them.

One important note: the resale value of diamonds is typically well below their retail purchase price. A diamond engagement ring bought at a jewelry store for $3,000 may be worth $600–$900 to a pawn shop — not because you're being underpaid, but because retail jewelry markup is extremely high. This is true across all pawn shops and jewelry buyers, not specific to any one dealer.

Common Myths About Pawn Shop Gold Prices

✗ Myth

"Pawn shops pay pennies on the dollar for gold."

✔ Reality

Reputable shops pay 70–85% of melt value for gold. "Pennies on the dollar" comes from comparing to retail jewelry price — not actual gold content value.

✗ Myth

"Brand name jewelry is worth more at a pawn shop."

✔ Reality

Most pawn shops buy gold by weight and purity, not brand. A Tiffany ring and a generic ring of the same weight and karat get the same gold offer. Exceptions exist for very high-end brands with strong collector demand.

✗ Myth

"Broken gold is worthless."

✔ Reality

Broken gold has full melt value. A snapped 14K chain gets the same offer per gram as an intact one. Don't throw away broken jewelry — bring it in.

✗ Myth

"I should get what I paid for it."

✔ Reality

Gold prices fluctuate. Retail jewelry includes markup for design, labor, and brand. A pawn shop buys for resale value — which is based on current gold content, not original purchase price.

How to Get the Most for Your Gold

These practical steps can meaningfully improve what you receive when you sell or pawn gold jewelry near Cincinnati:

  • Know what you have before you go. Check the karat stamp inside the band or on the clasp (14K, 585, 750, etc.). Knowing the karat tells you the purity without needing to be tested.
  • Bring everything. Combine all your gold pieces into one transaction — odd earrings, broken chains, old rings you never wear. Everything with gold content has value.
  • Group by karat. Keep your 14K pieces separate from 10K and 18K. This speeds up the appraisal and helps you see the value of each group clearly.
  • Ask to see the calculation. A reputable shop will show you the scale weight, confirm the karat test result, and show you the spot price they used. If a shop won't explain their offer, that's a problem.
  • Check the spot price yourself. Look up gold prices before you go. If you know gold is at $90/gram and your 10-gram 14K ring gets an offer of $150, you know something doesn't add up (should be ~$310 at 75% of melt value).
  • Get multiple offers if you have time. We encourage it. Fair dealers aren't afraid of competition. Bethel Pawn will match or beat any legitimate written offer from another licensed dealer.

Pawn Shop vs. Jewelry Store — Who Pays More?

A common question. The answer is: it depends on the specific jewelry store and the specific pawn shop.

Many jewelry stores will only buy back pieces they originally sold, and often at low percentages. Some have robust buying programs. The best way to compare is to get offers from multiple buyers — including Bethel Pawn — and see the numbers for yourself.

What pawn shops typically do better: buying gold by weight regardless of where it was purchased, buying broken and damaged pieces, and buying costume pieces that jewelry stores won't touch (as long as they have actual gold content).
