Best Video Game Consoles to Pawn

🎮 Consoles & Games ⏱ 7 min read 📍 Bethel Pawn, Bethel, Ohio

Not all consoles pawn equally. Current-gen hardware follows predictable market prices, but retro gaming is where surprises happen — both good and bad. Here's what a pawn shop actually looks for, and how to get the best offer on your system or game collection.

Video games are one of pawn shops' most consistent categories — demand is steady, inventory turns over quickly, and the resale market is well-established. But the range of values is enormous. A current-gen console in good condition with accessories is a straightforward transaction. A box of old cartridges from the 80s and 90s? That could be anywhere from $20 to a genuinely surprising number depending on what's in there.

This guide covers what Bethel Pawn looks for when evaluating video games and consoles for pawning or selling — and specifically, why retro gaming deserves more attention than most people give it.

Current-Gen Consoles: Predictable Value

The current generation — PlayStation 5, Xbox Series X/S, and Nintendo Switch — all have active, well-tracked resale markets. Pawn offers are based on what these units sell for right now, minus the shop's margin. A few things to know:

🎮 Current-Gen Pawn Checklist

  • Factory reset first. A console locked to your PSN, Xbox, or Nintendo account cannot be sold or used by anyone else. Factory reset before bringing it in. This is the single most important thing you can do.
  • Include the controller. A bare PS5 without a DualSense is worth less than a complete setup. All cables and the original controller matter.
  • Disc vs. Digital edition. Disc drives add value because they expand the new owner's options. Digital-only consoles pawn for slightly less.
  • Condition matters, but imperfection is fine. Normal scratches and wear don't kill an offer. A cracked housing or broken HDMI port does.

The Switch family — standard, Lite, and OLED — pawn consistently well because Nintendo's hardware holds value unusually well compared to competitors. The game card format also means the library travels with the handheld, which helps complete setups.

Last-Gen: Still Plenty of Value

PS4, Xbox One, and the Wii U are fully in "last-gen" territory now, but they still pawn for real money. The PS4 Pro and Xbox One X commands more than base models. The game libraries for these platforms are enormous and affordable — which means buyers for these systems are active.

A note on PS3 and Xbox 360: These are getting old enough that condition matters significantly more. A working PS3 slim in clean condition with controllers still gets a reasonable offer. A fat PS3 with YLOD issues gets almost nothing. The same applies to the 360's red ring era. Bring them in working.

Retro Gaming: The Wildcard Category

This is where things get genuinely interesting — and where most people either leave money on the table or make a costly mistake by throwing things out.

Retro gaming values have climbed dramatically over the past decade as the generation that grew up with NES, SNES, and Genesis hit their 30s and 40s with disposable income and nostalgia to spend. Games that retailed for $60 in 1994 can be worth hundreds today. Games that seemed ordinary are now out of print and scarce.

The "Box Full of Old Games" Scenario

This comes up all the time. Someone is cleaning out a parent's or grandparent's house and finds a box of cartridges from the 80s and 90s. The instinct is to assume they're worthless garage sale fodder. Please don't assume that.

Here are real examples of games that look ordinary but aren't:

  • Little Samson (NES) — looks like any other side-scroller. Worth $400–$1,200+ depending on condition.
  • Earthbound (SNES) — complete with box and guide: $200–$500+. Loose cart alone: $100–$200.
  • Conker's Bad Fur Day (N64) — rented constantly in 2001. Complete copy now $80–$150+.
  • Suikoden II (PS1) — common PS1 RPG at the time. Now $150–$300+ for a clean copy.
  • Panzer Dragoon Saga (Saturn) — 4-disc RPG. One of the most valuable games in existence: $600–$1,500+.

We're not suggesting every game box is hiding treasure — most aren't. But it costs you nothing to call us at (513) 427-4705 and describe what you have before you decide something is worthless.

Complete-in-Box vs. Loose: The Single Biggest Value Factor for Retro

For modern games, the box is a nice-to-have. For retro cartridges, it's transformative. A game that's worth $20 loose might be worth $80 complete with box and manual. An extremely rare title that's worth $300 loose can jump to $800+ complete-in-box.

If you have old games stored in original boxes, keep them together. Don't toss the box to save space. The manual matters too — for certain titles, a manual alone has collector value.

What Reduces Your Offer

A few things that hurt console and game pawn values more than people expect:

  • Account lock. A PS5 or Xbox Series X tied to an active account that can't be reset is nearly unsellable. Factory reset before you come in.
  • Missing power supply or HDMI. Every modern console should include these. Missing cables reduce offers.
  • Counterfeit cartridges. Bootleg NES, SNES, and GBA carts look identical to authentic ones at first glance. We check them. Fakes are worth nothing.
  • Disc rot. Some PS1 and PS2 discs from that era have developed disc rot and won't read properly. We test everything.
  • Broken analog sticks. Stick drift on DualShock or Joy-Cons is common and impacts value. Be upfront about it.

The Bottom Line

Current-gen consoles pawn at predictable market rates. Last-gen is still solid. Retro is where the real surprises live — both the pleasant kind (that box of old cartridges might be worth calling about) and the cautionary kind (don't throw things out before you know what you have).
